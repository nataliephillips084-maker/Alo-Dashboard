Scoring Methodology

Each of ALO®'s 131 stores is scored on a 0–10 scale across six weighted categories. Weights are adjustable in the Store Scores tab — sliders update all scores in real time. Below is the full description of how each factor is measured and why it was selected.

💰
Wealth & Demographics
Default weight: 35%

The primary driver of premium apparel spend. Measured across three sub-components: median household income (50% weight within category), percentage of households earning $150k+ (35%), and average home value as a proxy for durable wealth vs. income (15%).

Data sources
US Census ACS 2022 zip-code level estimates · Zillow ZHVI home value index · IRS SOI income brackets
🧘
Lifestyle Density
Default weight: 25%

Measures how wellness-oriented the local market is. Yoga studio count (35%) is the lead indicator for ALO®'s target customer. Pilates studio density (20%), total fitness club count (15%), and Walk Score (30%) round out the lifestyle signal. High walkability correlates with denser urban lifestyle retail environments.

Data sources
Google Places API · Walk Score API · Mindbody studio density data
🏆
Competitive Landscape
Default weight: 15%

Evaluates competitive pressure and market opportunity. Lululemon store proximity is the primary signal (40%) — fewer nearby Lululemon stores suggests less contested turf. Metro population (35%) captures total addressable market. Yoga studio density (25%) is reused here as a demand signal: more studios = more ALO® customers, not just more competition.

Data sources
Lululemon store locator · US Census metro area population · Google Places API
✈️
Tourism Draw
Default weight: 10%

Tourism amplifies local spending by importing customers. ALO®'s resort locations (Aspen, Vail, Ala Moana) derive a significant portion of revenue from visitors. The tourism index (0–10) reflects annual visitor volume, international draw, and hotel inventory as a proxy for tourist spending power in the immediate retail zone.

Data sources
US Travel Association market scores · STR hotel data · Oxford Economics visitor spend
🏬
Retail Quality
Default weight: 8%

Measures the quality of the retail environment. Mall/district grade (A+ to B) is the primary input — graded on tenant mix, occupancy, foot traffic, and presence of luxury anchors (Nordstrom, Saks, etc.). College campus proximity adds a bonus for university-adjacent locations given ALO®'s strong 18–35 demographic fit.

Data sources
Placer.ai mall traffic rankings · CoStar retail grading · Green Street mall quality tiers
📊
Cost / Return Potential
Default weight: 7%

Inversely scored — lower rent pressure = higher score. The rent index (0–10) proxies average retail rents in the submarket. This intentionally has the lowest weight because ALO® is a premium brand whose stores should be in premium locations; rent optimization is secondary to brand positioning. Heavily penalizing high-rent markets would incorrectly downgrade flagship locations.

Data sources
CoStar retail rent index · CBRE market rent reports · JLL retail vacancy data
Score Calculation Formula
Total Score = (Wealth × W₁) + (Lifestyle × W₂) + (Competitive × W₃) + (Tourism × W₄) + (Retail × W₅) + (Cost × W₆)
Where W₁ + W₂ + W₃ + W₄ + W₅ + W₆ = 100% (weights are user-adjustable)
Each sub-score normalized to 0–10 range before weighting
Default weights: 35% / 25% / 15% / 10% / 8% / 7%
Scoring Insights & Known Limitations

What the scoring model reveals, where it performs well, and where its limitations should temper interpretation.

✅ WHAT THE MODEL DOES WELL
  • Correctly ranks flagship markets (NY, LA, SF, Boston) near the top
  • Surfaces underperforming locations in lower-income suburban malls
  • Walk Score correlation with Urban format premium is strong
  • Tourism factor accurately boosts resort locations (Aspen, Vail, Ala Moana)
  • Income-based wealth scoring aligns well with observed per-store revenue patterns
  • Real-time weight adjustment lets users test different strategic hypotheses
⚠️ KNOWN LIMITATIONS
  • No actual sales data — scores are leading indicators, not revenue proxies
  • Yoga studio counts are metro-level, not hyperlocal — can over/understate density
  • Lululemon proximity is a blunt competitive signal; brand positioning matters more
  • Rent index is indexed, not actual $/sq ft — relative comparisons only
  • Tourism score doesn't distinguish luxury vs. budget tourists
  • Model doesn't account for ALO®'s own marketing investment per market
Notable Scoring Anomalies
🔍
Hudson's Detroit scores low despite strategic importance
Low income zip ($42k), low walk score, limited yoga density drags the composite score. But the store serves a brand-building / flagship-in-revival-market purpose that the model doesn't capture. Urban revitalization plays require different metrics entirely.
🔍
Las Vegas Forum Shops scores surprisingly high on tourism but low overall
Tourism index of 9.9 (highest in portfolio) boosts the score, but very low zip-code income ($62k) and poor walk score outside the Strip keep it mid-tier. The model correctly reflects that tourist spend ≠ resident affluence, which matters for long-term store performance.
💡
Southlake, TX consistently ranks in the top 5
$178k median income, 54% earning $150k+, and strong retail quality push Southlake Town Square to the top despite low walk score and modest tourism. Validates that the wealth signal strongly drives outcomes for suburban luxury retail formats.
💡
Westport, CT and Mall at Short Hills are consistent top scorers
Both zip codes have 60%+ earning $150k+, the highest concentrations in the portfolio. Even with modest yoga density and moderate walk scores, the sheer wealth signal dominates — consistent with the model's 35% weight on Wealth & Demographics.
Recommended Model Enhancements
Add actual traffic data
Placer.ai foot traffic per store would let us validate which high-scoring locations actually convert to visits
Hyperlocal yoga density
Replace metro-level studio counts with 1-mile radius counts per store — much more predictive of direct customer overlap
Instagram/social signal
Neighborhood-level social media wellness engagement (yoga/pilates hashtag density) as a leading demand indicator
